\section{Problem Statement and Contextualisation}

\quad In classical mythology, the labyrinth of Crete was navigated with \textit{"Ariadne’s thread,"} a lifeline provided by an outside observer to guide a vulnerable explorer safely through a complex, unknown maze. Modern autonomous robotics faces a similar problem. Unmanned Ground Vehicles (UGVs) used in search and rescue (SAR) or tactical situations typically rely on Global Positioning Systems (GPS) or active sensor emissions, such as LiDAR, to determine their location and plan navigable routes. \cite{11219502}. However, in highly contested or unstructured environments, such as disaster zones, deep forest canopies, or electronic warfare scenarios, these signals can often be degraded, obstructed, or intentionally jammed \cite{8741612}.

\vspace{0.5cm}

When active signals fail, autonomous ground navigation systems become functionally blind. To address this critical vulnerability, this project presents \textbf{Ariadne}: an autonomous \textbf{air-to-ground optical routing pipeline}. This system acts as a modern digital thread, utilising a single high-altitude RGB image captured by an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV). It passively maps the environment below, identifies navigable corridors, and calculates the optimal path for the ground asset.

\vspace{0.5cm}

To test the core logic of the computer vision and pathfinding modules without the interference of unpredictable lighting conditions or organic textures found in the real world, this study uses procedurally generated mazes as a synthetic proof-of-concept. The maze environment acts as a controlled sandbox to isolate and evaluate the core algorithmic pipeline before scaling up to more complex, unstructured terrains.

\vspace{0.5cm}

\subsection{System Requirements}

To successfully achieve optical-based autonomous routing, the proposed pipeline must fulfil the following sequential requirements:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Target Extraction:} The system must accurately detect and extract the pixel coordinates of the starting agent (the UGV) and the target destination (a human or objective) from the overhead image.

    \item \textbf{Environmental Segmentation:} The system must classify the environment, accurately dividing the image into strictly traversable (pathways) and non-traversable (walls/obstacles) terrain.

    \item \textbf{Tactical Pathfinding:} The system must compute an optimal, collision-free route from the start node to the target node using the segmented map, and project this route back onto the original visual space.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Literature Review}

The difficulty of autonomous navigation in environments without GPS has led to significant research into alternative localisation and mapping methods. 

\vspace{0.5cm}

Recent research on Unmanned Ground Vehicles (UGVs) has extensively explored the use of environment-specific, non-visual sensors to overcome the limitations posed by degraded optical conditions. The project applied \textbf{magnetic field map-based localisation}, which uses natural magnetic anomalies as navigational features \cite{11219502}. This approach employs Gaussian Process (GP) Regression to create high-resolution magnetic signature maps. It also combines a hybrid Particle Filter (PF) and Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF) to estimate the trajectory of UGVs. In tests conducted in indoor corridors, this methodology demonstrated remarkable resilience, reducing localisation errors by as much as 45\% compared to standard particle filter (PF)-based methods. However, this approach relies entirely on localised, ground-level sensing, meaning that the UGV must physically navigate and explore the environment to use the map effectively. \textbf{Ariadne} addresses this critical gap by changing the approach from \textit{"ground-level discovery"} to \textit{"overhead deterministic routing,"} enabling the UGV to receive an optimal path before encountering any obstacles.

\vspace{0.5cm}

Beyond ground-level localisation, extensive research has examined how Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) navigate in GPS-denied environments. A prominent framework proposed for UAV exploration combines Simultaneous Localisation and Mapping (\textbf{SLAM}) with Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (\textbf{POMDP}) \cite{8741612}. In this model, navigation is treated as a sequential decision problem under uncertainty. Simulation results showed that the UAV could safely navigate, avoid collisions, and maximise information gain while creating an occupancy map of an unknown environment. While effective for a single agent, this framework focuses solely on the UAV’s self-preservation and exploration. It does not consider the potential of utilising the generated occupancy map to actively guide a separate ground asset. \textbf{Ariadne} bridges this gap by using the UAV as an overhead optical \textit{"oracle"} that segments the terrain to guide the movements of a UGV.

\vspace{0.5cm}

To achieve optical terrain mapping, deep learning-based semantic segmentation has become the standard. Recent advancements in aerial road extraction utilise Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), particularly the \textbf{U-Net architecture}, to carry out binary semantic segmentation on remote sensing imagery \cite{Doghmane_Boukari_2024}. Research by Doghmane and Boukari demonstrates that enhancing the U-Net model with \textbf{Batch Group Normalisation} (BGN) significantly improves its ability to classify individual pixels into distinct object categories, such as road versus non-road areas. By dividing high-resolution aerial images into manageable patches and applying a sigmoid activation function to output a continuous pixel value, their BGN-UNet model achieved an exceptional Mean Intersection over Union (IoU) of 98.4\%. \textbf{Ariadne} takes direct inspiration from this pixel-wise classification approach. Just as BGN-UNet isolates navigable infrastructure from complex backgrounds, Ariadne utilises deep learning segmentation on overhead UAV imagery to divide simulated tactical environments (represented via procedural mazes) into strictly traversable paths and non-traversable boundaries. However, while current literature primarily treats the segmented image as the final output, Ariadne expands on this idea by utilising the high-IoU segmentation mask as a foundational grid for \textbf{A* pathfinding}. This approach translates the visual classification into a mathematical route for a ground agent.

\vspace{0.5cm}

\section{Design and Methodology}

The system architecture of Ariadne consists of four sequential phases: synthetic data generation, target extraction, environmental segmentation, and tactical pathfinding. This pipeline was implemented using a combination of Blender 5.1 for procedural environment modelling, and PyTorch (U-Net) and Ultralytics (YOLO) for computer vision processing.

\vspace{0.5cm}

\subsection{Synthetic Data Generation}

\quad The basis of this proof-of-concept is to create a highly controlled and noiseless environment for the independent evaluation of the algorithmic logic in the computer vision and pathfinding modules. Before deploying optical routing architectures into unstructured, highly occluded real-world topographies (such as forest canopies or disaster zones), the core logic must be validated.

\vspace{0.5cm}

To achieve this, a custom dataset of procedural 2D mazes was generated using the Blender Python API (\texttt{bpy}). Utilising a 3D rendering engine (Figure~\ref{fig:blender_viewport}) for top-down 2D maze generation enables automated extraction of mathematically perfect ground-truth labels.

\vspace{0.5cm}

\hspace{-1.5em}For each procedural iteration, the rendering pipeline was configured to automatically export three distinct assets:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{RGB Overhead Image:} A colour rendering (Figure~\ref{fig:rgb-1}) simulating the raw optical feed captured by the UAV's downward-facing camera.

    \item \textbf{Binary Segmentation Mask:} A grayscale image (Figure~\ref{fig:bsm-1}) mapping the precise pixel coordinates of paths (white) against walls (black). This serves as the ground truth for the U-Net architecture.

    \item \textbf{Bounding Box Annotations:} A text file (Listing~\ref{lst:bbox_output}) containing the normalised spatial coordinates and class identifiers for the starting agent (0: UGV) and the target objective (1: Human). These labels adhere to the YOLO formatting standard, providing the ground truth required for the target extraction phase.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Target Extraction}

\quad Before an autonomous route can be calculated, the system must first determine the spatial coordinates of the start and end points within the unknown environment. In a real-world search and rescue (SAR) or tactical scenario, these points represent the Unmanned Ground Vehicle (the starting point) and the human target (the destination).

\vspace{0.5cm}

To achieve this, \textit{Ariadne} uses a custom-trained object detection model built on the YOLO (You Only Look Once) framework. YOLO was chosen for this phase because of its advanced inference speed and high bounding box precision, which are essential for real-time tactical applications. The model is trained to analyse raw RGB overhead images captured by the UAV and to isolate the specific pixel clusters representing the UGV (Unmanned Ground Vehicle) and human subjects (Figure~\ref{fig:yolo_sample}). The training process utilised the Ultralytics framework and was optimised for the specific resolution of the synthetic dataset ($640\times640$px), as outlined in Listing~\ref{lst:yolo_train}.

\subsubsection{Spatial Discretisation}

\quad After successfully drawing bounding boxes over the agents, the continuous visual space must be converted into a discrete mathematical format that is suitable for classical pathfinding algorithms. This spatial discretisation is inspired by the approach to aerial segmentation developed by Doghmane and Boukari \cite{Doghmane_Boukari_2024}. In their research, high-resolution remote sensing imagery was systematically divided into fixed $256\times256$ spatial patches. This technique maximised computational efficiency while preserving important spatial relationships.

\newpage

\hspace{-1.5em}\textit{Ariadne} applies this philosophy by downsampling the continuous optical feed into a grid defined by one main parameter (\texttt{grid\_size}). From this parameter, the spatial constraints are mathematically derived:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Image Size:} The raw pixel resolution of the square overhead optical input (e.g., $640\times640$).

    \item \textbf{Grid Size:} The primary defined parameter establishing the matrix dimensions (e.g., $30\times30$).

    \item \textbf{Cell Size:} A derived value calculated by dividing the \texttt{img\_size} by the \texttt{grid\_size}, representing the exact pixel span of a single grid cell.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.5cm}

To turn the YOLO bounding boxes into usable grid nodes, the extraction module finds the exact centre pixel of each bounding box. It then divides these pixel coordinates by the cell size and rounds them to whole numbers to find the specific $(x, y)$ grid index where the agent is located. Finally, a bounding function ensures these values stay within the limits of the matrix array. This process is shown in Listing~\ref{lst:spatial_discretisation}.

\vspace{0.5cm}

\noindent\begin{minipage}{1\linewidth}
    \begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption=Translating visual bounding boxes into bounded discrete grid nodes, label=lst:spatial_discretisation]
# Calculate dimensions
px_w = x2 - x1
px_h = y2 - y1

# Calculate exact center pixel
px_x = x1 + (px_w // 2)
px_y = y1 + (px_h // 2)

# Convert pixel centers to grid indices for pathfinding
grid_x = int(px_x // self.cell_size)
grid_y = int(px_y // self.cell_size)

# Clamp values to ensure they never exceed grid bounds
grid_x = max(0, min(self.grid_size - 1, grid_x))
grid_y = max(0, min(self.grid_size - 1, grid_y))
    \end{lstlisting}
\end{minipage}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\subsubsection{Grid Resolution Tuning}

\quad The selection of the grid size parameter is a critical optimisation problem that directly dictates both the computational efficiency and the physical safety of the autonomous route.

\vspace{0.5cm}

\hspace{-1.5em}Tuning the grid resolution requires balancing two competing extremes:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Low Resolution (Under-sampling):} A smaller grid size significantly reduces the dimensionality of the search matrix, leading to exceptionally fast A* execution times. However, if the grid resolution is too low, the system suffers from \textbf{spatial aliasing}. Thin boundary walls and traversable pathways are combined into the same discrete cell, leading the algorithm to falsely classify safe routes as blocked and frequently resulting in a "no route found" error.
  
  \item \textbf{High Resolution (Over-sampling):} Conversely, a larger grid size heavily increases the complexity of the matrix, driving up the computational time required for the pathfinding module to solve the route. Furthermore, in a highly granular grid, the A* algorithm's mathematical optimisation will inherently draw the shortest possible path, which often results in the route \textbf{"hugging" the absolute edges} of walls and corners. For a physical UGV, this lack of spatial tolerance introduces a severe risk of mechanical collision.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.5cm}

To achieve a balance between computational latency and navigational safety, \textit{Ariadne} uses a proportional tuning strategy informed by the known dimensions of its procedural environment. Since the synthetic mazes are created on a basic $10\times10$ logical grid, the optical pathfinding matrix has been specifically adjusted to a $30\times30$ resolution.

\vspace{0.5cm}

This establishes a precise $1:9$ spatial mapping, where each cell of the original maze corresponds to a $3\times3$ block of cells in the pathfinding matrix. This specific ratio ensures that the thin boundary walls occupy separate grid nodes from the traversable corridors. It provides an adequate spatial buffer to prevent the pathfinder from clipping the edges of the walls, completely eliminates the risk of path occlusion, and maintains a sufficiently lightweight matrix for fast algorithmic execution.
